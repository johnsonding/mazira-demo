How Small is Big Data

by Anith Mathai
2013/08/07

Big data is quite the buzzword these days. The original definition was meant to signify data that was coming in too fast (high velocity), was too voluminous and highly diversified, for an organization to handle. So the size is really relative, one organization’s big data may be another’s 'small data'. The term Big Data was originally coined to represent datasets that were too big for commonly used software tools to handle. But industry seems to have forgotten the origins.

From a marketing perspective, industry would like you to think that ‘Big Data’ has to be astronomical in proportion. In fact there are some definitions available on the internet that define big data in terms of a volume, over 1TB etc. This is probably due to the fact that the need to handle big data came from the astronomy field. The research telescopes and satellites were collecting gargantuan amounts of data that the existing relational databases (like SQL) etc were finding it difficult to handle. The term Big data evolved, probably from some astronomy dabbler. I think the word ‘Big’ in ‘Big Data’, is probably an inside reference to the Big Bang theory rather than ‘big’ in terms of volume, but, hey that is just my opinion.

My take on the evolution of Big Data (oh wow, evolution and Big Bang in the same article!) is the fact that organizations like Walmart were processing millions of transactions every day, and some data ‘genius’ at the company noticed trends. Maybe they saw that stores in different areas were showing different sales trends on food sales, and then used a data service to map credit card users to demographics. So while most merchants complain that credit card companies charge fees that eat away at their margins, I would say the more credit card transactions you have as a retailer, the better you can understand your customer (plus most people I know would prefer to use plastic).

Still almost all data is pretty much useless, just like a big rock. It has to be transformed into useful information before it has value. Data is analogous to the rock which contains the ore. The ore is the information, and the intelligence that can be derived from the information is the metal in the ore. Handling the data and transforming it is akin to the mining industry. There are a number of ways to extract the rocks and the ore and ultimately the metal, but the highest value is the most efficient method of extraction. The next gold boom is Data!
