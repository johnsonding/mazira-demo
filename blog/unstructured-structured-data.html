Unstructured and Structured Data

by Anith Mathai
2013/09/30

“There are 10 kinds of people in this world, those who understand binary and those who do not”- Famous programmer saying.

There are also two kinds of data in this world- structured and unstructured. To easily explain these two kinds of data, take the analogy of a football stadium. Outside the stadium is a large crowd. This crowd is highly unorganized, walking in different directions, doing different things, eating, yelling etc. Now the same crowd walks into the stadium and takes their seats. All of a sudden they are organized, sitting in different sections. They all stand for the national anthem, do the stadium wave. They even hold up the cardboard placard at the right time for those cool stadium graphics. Hard to imagine the same group of people were completely disorganized outside.

Unstructured data is the chaotic crowd outside the stadium; structured data is the well-behaved crowd inside the stadium. Unstructured data is meant for human consumption, tweets, Facebook posts, Word documents, emails etc. Getting information out of unstructured data generally means you would read it and utilize your human brain to understand it. Structured documents on the other hand are meant for the computer, SQL databases, Oracle, SAP etc., most humans would not really be able to make sense of a database. To really get information out of a structured database, you would, most likely, have to be a programmer or would need a programmer to help you.

Structured data is the stuff that most of the general population never really encounters. It is one of those things that is seen but rarely heard. Terms like SQL, query, relational database, etc etc, are not in common parlance. Yet this is exactly the kind of data that powers many applications that we use on a daily basis. The strict structure of these databases makes it very easy to apply computer logic. The fact that it follows highly logical rules makes it perfect for computers. Structured databases are ideal for holding large amounts of the same kind of information and performing similar operations.

The data disconnect between structured and unstructured data can be traced back to early computers, which were designed to perform logical operations and repetitive actions. In the early days of computing the user interfaces were tape and punch cards. You only used a computer if you were a programmer. Fast-forward a few decades and practically the whole world uses computers. The combination of word processing advancements (MS Word, blogs, web editors) and point and click interfaces made it very easy for the general population to generate vast amounts of data. Now, this data is meant for human consumption, which means that it is unstructured. The data processing systems however are still structured. End result: vast amounts of information are just hidden in unstructured data.

Ho hum… big deal, why should I care? Well do you care about your bottom line? How much does your organization spend on an annual basis to read customer complaints, tweets and documents? Then there is the very expensive phase of converting unstructured data into structured data. You probably don’t realize it,but a huge amount of your employee’s time is spent searching for information in unstructured files. Google in a white paper titled ”Return on Information”, estimated that the average knowledge worker spends close to 10 hours a week organizing and analyzing data.
