When OCR is Just Not Enough...

by Anith Mathai
2013/11/27

Optical Character Recognition (OCR), the science of recognizing characters from printed font has been around for a while. The roots of OCR can be traced back to helping the visually impaired read, with devices such as the optophone. I’m focusing more on the current, but you can read the interesting history of OCR at the OCR wiki page.

OCR has come a long way since the early stages of mass digitization during the 70’s and the 80’s. The focus back then was on converting the mounds of paper documents into digital documents. Character recognition technology was developed to read various printed font styles. The general idea was, scan in the paper document and then perform an OCR on the scanned document. Voilà, like magic, you now had the digital text, which you could store, transmit or reproduce. More importantly, the text is digitally available to be transferred to a database (more on that later…), essentially the digitized data could now be structured.

Although, the text was available digitally, the structuring process was still done manually by a human operator. (Read article on structured and unstructured for more info). Even with the manual intervention, this digitization process was still very efficient since a digital version of the text was now available, without having to re-key the whole document.

Check processing is probably one of the best examples of structuring OCR data. Bank checks are imprinted with magnetic ink (MICR) on the bottom strip that special readers can recognize. The information contained in the magnetic line is usually the bank routing number, check number, and account number. A human operator includes the amount, which is also encoded on the MICR strip, using special magnetic printers. As the check passes through the magnetic reader, all these encoded elements are transmitted into a central database, which banks uses to reconcile accounts within the bank as well as other financial institutions.

In recent years there has been a shift from MICR systems, to light based OCR systems, which can recognize the special characters from images, without having to read it “magnetically”. This system allows you to deposit a picture of your check, instead of the actual check.

Almost anyone can attest to the efficiency of the federal check processing system, but, when it comes to larger documents like HUD documents, immigration forms, tax returns, etc., extracting data and structuring seems to hit a roadblock. Why? Well one reason is there is no special ink or font being used. But, more importantly, the data that has to be structured is spread all over the place, in different columns; different input formats (check boxes, address boxes etc.). A simple OCR will just output text. The real value is not in digitizing the document, but structuring the data from the document into a database. Even today this structuring process is done manually for the most part.
